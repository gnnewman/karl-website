Understanding the diagnosis

What is leptomeningeal disease?

A lot of people have asked what Karl is facing, so we've developed an "FAQ" on his condition here.

Understanding LMD

Leptomeningeal disease (LMD) is when cancer cells reach the leptomeninges, the thin layers of tissue that wrap around the brain and spinal cord, and the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) that flows around them.

Because that fluid circulates everywhere the brain and spine reach, the disease can touch many areas at once. That's why symptoms can seem scattered or change from week to week. Karl's primary symptoms are confusion, short-term memory loss, and difficulty walking.

How Karl developed LMD

In Karl's case, it came from his non-Hodgkin lymphoma. His lymphoma spread to his leptomeninges, the layers of tissue surrounding the brain and spinal cord. The lining around the brain is partly shielded by the blood-brain barrier. Because that barrier protects the brain, this area is very hard for treatments to reach, which is part of what makes LMD so difficult.

Karl was officially diagnosed with LMD after a spinal tap revealed malignant cells in his CSF.

The course of the disease

LMD is considered an advanced stage of cancer. It tends to affect several parts of the nervous system, and the day-to-day picture can shift. Because of that, care usually focuses on comfort, dignity, and quality of life, easing specific symptoms rather than aiming for a cure.

Prognosis

LMD is serious, and survival is often measured in weeks to months. We are choosing to be present at home, keep him comfortable, and make the time we have as fulfilling as possible.

What hospice means for us

Karl's hospice team helps manage pain and symptoms, supports him at home, and supports our whole family. It lets us spend our energy on being with him.
